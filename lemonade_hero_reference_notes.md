# Lemonade hero reference notes

The Lemonade homepage hero does **not** rely on a literal 3D object. Its impact comes from a very clean editorial headline centered in the viewport, a bright and spacious background, and illustrated scene elements pushed to the left and right edges so the center stays clear for messaging.

The motion impression is subtle rather than cinematic in a heavy, glossy sense. The page feels animated because the hero behaves like a staged world: soft line-art environments, lightweight character illustration, layered depth at the edges, and restrained transitions around the call to action. The design reads as premium because the composition is highly controlled, not because it uses complex real-time 3D rendering.

For Cardone, the best adaptation is **not** to copy Lemonade’s white illustration style directly. The transferable idea is the **hero architecture**: a simple, dominant headline area in the middle, a strong focal asset that frames rather than blocks the copy, and a background treatment that suggests dimension and motion without overwhelming performance.

The Cardone version should translate this into a darker, black-and-gold language using a cinematic motion asset or faux-3D scene, with the central copy remaining calm and legible while the media creates atmosphere around it.

A DOM inspection of the Lemonade homepage hero showed **no `<video>` elements, no `<canvas>` elements, and no detectable Lottie player in the hero viewport**. The effect appears to be constructed from layered illustration and SVG-based decorative assets rather than an actual 3D video background. That means the Cardone redesign should focus on recreating the **perceived depth and polish** of the experience, not on copying a specific hidden 3D pipeline.

## Adapted Cardone hero concept

The new Cardone hero should borrow Lemonade’s **clarity of composition** rather than its aesthetic. The Cardone version will keep the headline zone centered and spacious, then frame it with a premium motion scene that feels like a dark insurance vault opening into a high-value world. Instead of simple line art, the Cardone experience should use a refined moving backdrop with metallic highlights, controlled parallax, and a sculptural focal object or scene panel that suggests dimensionality.

The intended result is a hero that feels more cinematic and expensive than the current version, while still preserving the same conversion structure: immediate brand recognition, a strong primary quote CTA, secondary exploration, and enough empty space for the typography to stay elegant over motion.
